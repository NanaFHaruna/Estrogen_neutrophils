 ### Estrogen imprints neutrophil fate through epigenetic programming of hematopoiesis underlying female-biased autoimmunity

<h2>Description</h2>
This project sought to understand mechanisms underlying sex differences in neutrophils, which likely impact sex differences in susceptibility to autoimmunity, infection, and cancer. We are performing single-cell RNA and single-cell ATAC sequencing of human and mouse neutrophils to assess for sex hormone and/or X-chromosome-mediated transcriptional changes.
<br />

<h2>Abstract</h2>
Neutrophils are essential mediators of host defense and increasingly recognized contributors to female-predominant autoimmune diseases. Although sex differences in neutrophil maturation, metabolism, and type I interferon (IFN) responses have been described, the mechanisms linking biological sex to neutrophil function remain poorly understood. Here, using complementary studies in wild-type and transgenic mice together with analyses of healthy individuals, patients with androgen insensitivity syndrome, and patients with systemic lupus erythematosus, we identify estrogen as a central regulator of neutrophil development and inflammatory potential. Estrogen acts during hematopoiesis to program neutrophil fate through extensive epigenetic remodeling of hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells and early neutrophil precursors. Acting through estrogen receptor α, it reshapes chromatin accessibility and enhancer architecture, establishing a latent transcriptional program that promotes the emergence of a distinct population of type I IFN-responsive neutrophils with heightened inflammatory potential. Mechanistically, estrogen redirects neutrophil maturation trajectories and enhances responsiveness to type I IFNs through epigenetic priming of key signaling pathways, including STAT1, IRF1, IRF7, and IKKε. This developmental program is conserved in humans, independent of sex chromosome complement, and persists in autoimmune disease. Importantly, selective depletion of IFN-responsive neutrophils attenuates lupus-like disease in vivo, establishing their pathogenic role in autoimmunity. Together, these findings uncover a previously unrecognized mechanism by which estrogen imprints innate immune cell identity during hematopoiesis, providing a developmental framework linking biological sex to immune function and disease susceptibility.
